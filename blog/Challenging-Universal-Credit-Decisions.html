Universal Credit is often presented as a straightforward safety net. Fill in the forms, report your circumstances honestly, provide evidence if asked, and the system will work as intended.

That has not been my experience.

My family’s journey through Universal Credit has involved repeated challenges around capital, misunderstandings about beneficial ownership, pressure to communicate in ways that worsened my health, and a constant need to insist on reasonable adjustments that should never have required a fight in the first place.

What I learned through all of this is simple:

If you do not challenge bad decisions, those decisions can become “facts” in the system.

And if you are vulnerable, exhausted, or unwell, the system can quietly wear you down until you stop resisting.

This is why I want other claimants to understand something important:

You are allowed to push back.
You are allowed to ask questions.
You are allowed to demand evidence, clarity, and lawful decision-making.

And you do not have to do it alone.

—

The Myth of “The Computer Says No”

One of the most frustrating parts of dealing with Universal Credit is the way decisions can be presented as automatic or inevitable.

At various points, I was effectively treated as though all money in shared accounts belonged entirely to me, despite the reality that the funds involved included money belonging to my partner and daughter.

This matters enormously under Universal Credit rules because capital levels directly affect entitlement.

At first, it felt impossible to challenge. The system seemed confident. Officials referred to balances as though ownership was self-evident.

But when I carefully examined the regulations, gathered evidence, and methodically reconstructed transactions, the picture changed.

Eventually, the decision maker accepted that:
• my daughter and partner had beneficial ownership of part of the funds,
• the entire balance should not be treated as my capital,
• and previous calculations had been wrong.

Those corrections dramatically changed the outcome.

That did not happen because the system volunteered to reconsider itself.

It happened because I challenged the assumptions.

Precision Matters More Than Emotion

One of the biggest lessons I learned is that emotional arguments rarely move systems.

Evidence does.

Precision does.

Timelines do.

Clear wording does.

When communicating through the Universal Credit journal, every sentence matters. A poorly worded message can accidentally create confusion or weaken your position. A carefully written message can force decision makers to engage with the actual issue.

Over time, I started treating journal entries almost like legal submissions:
• separating issues clearly,
• using headings,
• maintaining timelines,
• requesting explicit confirmation,
• asking direct questions,
• and documenting failures to respond.

This became especially important when dealing with claim reviews and requests for bank statements.

At one point, I repeatedly requested updates while waiting for documents that had still not arrived in the post. The lack of engagement itself became part of the issue.

I learned that silence from the Department for Work and Pensions can matter just as much as what they actually say.

If they are not responding, document that.
If they ignore reasonable concerns, document that.
If they fail to answer direct questions, document that too.

Reasonable Adjustments Are Not “Special Treatment”

Another major battle involved reasonable adjustments.

People with health conditions are often subtly pressured into handling things in ways that worsen their condition:
• unexpected phone calls,
• unclear demands,
• rushed deadlines,
• verbal discussions with no written confirmation,
• or repeated requests for information already provided.

I had to repeatedly insist on communication methods that were manageable for my health.

That was not me being difficult.

That was me insisting on rights protected under the Equality Act 2010.

Too many claimants feel guilty for asking for adjustments. They worry they will appear uncooperative.

But there is a difference between refusing to engage and asking to engage in a way that is accessible.

The DWP is a public authority.
That matters.

Public bodies have legal obligations.

If your health condition affects memory, concentration, fatigue, anxiety, communication, executive functioning, or your ability to cope with stress, you are entitled to ask for reasonable adjustments.

And you should ask for them clearly, calmly, and in writing.

The Importance of Slowing Down

Universal Credit processes are designed in a way that often creates urgency:
• upload this by a deadline,
• respond immediately,
• attend this appointment,
• explain this discrepancy,
• provide this evidence again.

When you are exhausted or frightened, it is easy to panic and send rushed replies.

That is dangerous.

One of the most valuable things I started doing was slowing down and thinking carefully before responding.

That is where ChatGPT became genuinely transformative for me.

Not because it “fought” the DWP for me.
Not because it replaced legal advice.
And not because it magically solved problems.

But because it helped me think clearly.

Using ChatGPT as a Strategic Tool

I started using ChatGPT almost like a second brain:
• checking whether my reasoning made sense,
• organising timelines,
• identifying contradictions,
• drafting careful journal messages,
• rewriting emotional responses into precise factual statements,
• and helping me understand regulations and procedures.

When you are dealing with fatigue, stress, cognitive overload, or brain fog, that support can make an enormous difference.

Instead of posting reactive messages while upset, I could stop and ask:
• Is this wording precise?
• Am I accidentally conceding something?
• Have I answered the actual question?
• What evidence supports this?
• What information is missing?
• What regulations might apply?

That process changed everything.

The quality of my communication improved dramatically.

And interestingly, the better organised and more precise my submissions became, the more seriously they appeared to be taken.

Challenging the “Power Imbalance”

Universal Credit can feel intimidating because there is such an obvious imbalance of power.

They have:
• departments,
• decision makers,
• legal teams,
• procedures,
• systems,
• and institutional confidence.

Meanwhile, claimants are often ill, exhausted, isolated, or financially desperate.

But here is something important I learned:

A surprising number of decisions are vulnerable to careful scrutiny.

Not because staff are evil.
Not because every decision is malicious.
But because systems make mistakes constantly.

Assumptions get made.
Evidence gets overlooked.
Context disappears.
People misunderstand regulations.
Computer systems flatten complicated human realities into simplistic categories.

That means informed, organised claimants are far more powerful than they realise.

Keep Records of Everything

If I could give one practical piece of advice to every claimant, it would be this:

Keep records obsessively.

Save journal entries.
Download statements.
Keep screenshots.
Record dates.
Maintain timelines.
Store PDFs.
Preserve letters.

Do not rely on memory.

Months later, tiny details can become critically important.

The difference between winning and losing a challenge can sometimes come down to:
• one sentence,
• one date,
• one missing clarification,
• or one overlooked piece of evidence.

You Are Allowed to Fight Back

Many claimants internalise the idea that if Universal Credit says something, it must be correct.

That is not true.

Decision makers can revise decisions.
Mandatory reconsiderations can succeed.
Tribunals overturn DWP decisions every day.

And sometimes the simple act of calmly refusing to accept an incorrect assumption changes the entire direction of a case.

What matters is persistence.

Not aggression.
Not abuse.
Not conspiracy theories.

Persistence.
Documentation.
Precision.
Evidence.
Clarity.

And the willingness to keep going when the system hopes you will give up.

Final Thoughts

This experience changed the way I see bureaucracy, disability, and advocacy.

I learned that vulnerable people are often expected to navigate highly technical systems while exhausted and unwell.

I also learned that careful thinking is a form of power.

For me, ChatGPT became part of that process — not as a replacement for human judgement, but as a tool for structuring thoughts, reducing confusion, and helping transform emotional overwhelm into clear communication.

If you are struggling with Universal Credit right now:
• slow down,
• document everything,
• question assumptions,
• insist on reasonable adjustments,
• and do not be afraid to challenge decisions that do not make sense.

You may be far more capable of fighting back than you realise.
