Why the Majorana Equation Preserves Handedness

1. Chirality

\[ \psi_L = \frac{1-\gamma^5}{2}\psi, \qquad \psi_R = \frac{1+\gamma^5}{2}\psi \] \[ \gamma^5 \psi_L = -\psi_L, \qquad \gamma^5 \psi_R = +\psi_R \] ---

2. Gamma matrices flip chirality

\[ \{\gamma^5, \gamma^\mu\} = 0 \] ---

3. The derivative term

\[ i\slashed{\partial}\psi_L = i\gamma^\mu \partial_\mu \psi_L \] \[ \gamma^5 (i\slashed{\partial}\psi_L) = + i\slashed{\partial}\psi_L \] So this is right-handed. ---

4. Charge conjugation

\[ \psi_c = \gamma^2 \psi^* \] \[ \gamma^2 (\gamma^5)^* = -\gamma^5 \gamma^2 \] \[ \psi_L \rightarrow (\psi_L)_c = (\psi_c)_R \] ---

5. Dirac vs Majorana

Dirac

\[ i\slashed{\partial}\psi = m\psi \] \[ i\slashed{\partial}\psi_L = m\psi_R \] Mixes chirality. ---

Majorana

\[ i\slashed{\partial}\psi = m\psi_c \] \[ i\slashed{\partial}\psi_L = m(\psi_L)_c \] Both sides are right-handed. ---

6. Conclusion

The equation closes within a single Weyl field.
