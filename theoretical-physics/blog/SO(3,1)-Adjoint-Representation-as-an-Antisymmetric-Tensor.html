Showing that for \(SO(3,1)\) the adjoint is the two-index antisymmetric tensor

For \(SO(3,1)\), the adjoint representation is naturally identified with the space of antisymmetric rank-2 tensors.

1. Start from the definition of the Lie algebra

The group \(SO(3,1)\) is the set of linear transformations preserving the Minkowski metric

\[ \eta = \mathrm{diag}(1,-1,-1,-1), \]

so its elements satisfy

\[ \Lambda^T \eta \Lambda = \eta. \]

The Lie algebra \(so(3,1)\) consists of infinitesimal transformations. Write

\[ \Lambda = I + \omega, \]

with \(\omega\) infinitesimal. Substituting into

\[ \Lambda^T \eta \Lambda = \eta \]

gives

\[ (I+\omega)^T \eta (I+\omega)=\eta. \]

Keeping only first-order terms,

\[ \omega^T \eta + \eta \omega = 0. \]

In index notation this is

\[ \eta_{\mu\rho}\,\omega^\rho{}_{\nu}+\eta_{\nu\rho}\,\omega^\rho{}_{\mu}=0. \]

Now lower an index:

\[ \omega_{\mu\nu} \equiv \eta_{\mu\rho}\,\omega^\rho{}_{\nu}. \]

Then the condition becomes

\[ \omega_{\mu\nu} + \omega_{\nu\mu}=0, \]

so

\[ \omega_{\mu\nu} = -\omega_{\nu\mu}. \]

Thus an element of the Lie algebra \(so(3,1)\) is exactly an antisymmetric 2-index object.

2. Count dimensions

In 4 dimensions, an antisymmetric tensor \(A_{\mu\nu}\) has

\[ \binom{4}{2}=6 \]

independent components. Also,

\[ \dim so(3,1)=\frac{4(4-1)}{2}=6. \]

So the Lie algebra has the same dimension as the space of antisymmetric 2-tensors.

3. Why this is the adjoint representation

The adjoint representation is the action of the group on its own Lie algebra:

\[ X \mapsto \Lambda X \Lambda^{-1}, \qquad X \in so(3,1). \]

If we write \(X\) with lowered indices as \(X_{\mu\nu}=-X_{\nu\mu}\), then under a Lorentz transformation it becomes

\[ X'_{\mu\nu} = \Lambda_\mu{}^\rho \, \Lambda_\nu{}^\sigma \, X_{\rho\sigma}. \]

That is exactly how a rank-2 tensor transforms, and since antisymmetry is preserved,

\[ X'_{\mu\nu} = -X'_{\nu\mu}, \]

it transforms specifically as an antisymmetric rank-2 tensor. So the adjoint action of \(SO(3,1)\) on \(so(3,1)\) is precisely the tensor transformation law for an antisymmetric 2-tensor.

4. Basis form

A standard basis for the Lie algebra is \(M_{\mu\nu}\), with

\[ M_{\mu\nu}=-M_{\nu\mu}, \]

so there are 6 generators:

\[ M_{01},\, M_{02},\, M_{03},\, M_{12},\, M_{13},\, M_{23}. \]

Any Lie algebra element can be written as

\[ X=\frac12 \, \omega^{\mu\nu} M_{\mu\nu}, \qquad \omega^{\mu\nu}=-\omega^{\nu\mu}. \]

Again, the coefficients \(\omega^{\mu\nu}\) are antisymmetric in two indices, showing the adjoint is carried by antisymmetric 2-index objects.

5. Relation to rotations and boosts

These 6 components split into

3 boost generators: \(M_{0i}\)
3 rotation generators: \(M_{ij}\)

So the antisymmetric tensor \(A_{\mu\nu}\) contains exactly the \(3+3\) degrees of freedom expected for the Lorentz algebra.

The key idea is that preserving the Minkowski metric forces an infinitesimal Lorentz generator to be antisymmetric after lowering one index with \(\eta_{\mu\nu}\). That is why the Lie algebra, and hence the adjoint representation, is the antisymmetric two-index tensor representation.

Conclusion

For \(SO(3,1)\), the Lie algebra consists of matrices satisfying

\[ X^T\eta+\eta X=0, \]

which is equivalent to saying

\[ X_{\mu\nu}=-X_{\nu\mu}. \]

So the Lie algebra is the space of antisymmetric rank-2 tensors, and since the adjoint representation is the group acting on its Lie algebra, the adjoint representation is the two-index antisymmetric tensor representation.
